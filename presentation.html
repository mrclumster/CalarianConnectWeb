02 — The Project

BarangayConnect modernizes how citizens transact with their barangay.

It's a unified system pairing a citizen mobile app, a staff web panel, and an AI voice pipeline — so requesting a Certificate of Residency or Indigency feels as easy as sending a message. My ownership area was the mobile app: the surface citizens actually touch.

The Problem
Right now, getting a barangay document means waking up early, lining up at the barangay hall, filling out paper forms, and often coming back another day. It takes hours — sometimes a whole morning.
What We Built
A free mobile app citizens can install on their phone. They request the document they need from home, in either Tagalog or English, and even just by speaking — no typing required.
My Part
I built the mobile app — the part the citizen actually holds in their hand. Sign-in, the request forms, the profile page, dark mode for night use, and the voice feature.
Tools Used
The app is built with React Native (so it works on Android phones) and connected to Whisper and Claude — the AI tools that listen to the citizen's voice and turn it into a filled-out form.
How It Should Feel
Simple, calm, and respectful. It should work on cheaper phones, read clearly day or night, and speak the citizen's own language so no one feels left out.
Where I Fit In
The project has four big stages. My work covered the first three — getting people signed in, building the main app, and starting the voice-to-form feature.
A Real Screenshot · The Home Page

This is what a citizen sees when they open the app.

At the top, a friendly greeting using their name and their barangay. Two Languages

Tagalog and English Together

I made sure every word in the app could show in both Tagalog and English. Each time I added a new screen, I added the translation right away — so the app never showed half-Tagalog, half-English text. Citizens can read it in whichever language they prefer.

Safe and Simple Sign-In

I built the way citizens log in. They first pick their barangay, then the app finds their existing record from the office's list, and finally they confirm it's really them with a one-time code sent to their phone — like the codes banks send.

I added a microphone button so citizens can just talk to the app instead of typing — helpful for older folks or anyone in a hurry. The app listens, understands what they said in Tagalog or English, and fills out the form for them automatically.

05 — Key Learnings

Four lessons that I'll carry past this internship.

Small commits beat heroic ones

A "fix the tab bar" commit takes ten minutes. A "rebuild the landing" commit takes a day. Both are valid — but I learned to know which one I was about to make and to write the message accordingly.

Design is a feature, not a finishing touch

The two UI sweeps weren't cosmetic — they're what made the app feel trustworthy. A citizen requesting an Indigency certificate needs to feel that the system respects them. That's a design outcome before it's an engineering one.

Code aligns to the real model

Removing face-enroll.tsx from the mobile app was the right call because face enrollment is a kiosk concern. The same instinct drove the auth overhaul to match the bulk-import identity model. Code should reflect the system, not fight it.

Accessibility starts with language

Shipping every UI change with paired Tagalog strings forced me to think about who the user actually is. English-first is a choice that excludes most of our users — bilingual-by-default is a choice that includes them.

06 — Reflection on the Process

Where I started, where I ended, and what I'd do differently.

Where I started

Capable, but unfocused

Day one, I could write a screen but I couldn't tell you why a screen should exist. I confused activity with progress. The first week's commits show that — they're enthusiastic but scattered.

Where I ended

Intentional, end-to-end

By Week 4, I was making structural calls — removing the obsolete face-enroll route, restructuring auth to match the real identity model, propagating dark mode in a single coherent pass. The commits got bigger because the thinking got tighter.

What I'd do differently

Plan, then sweep

I'd front-load planning — write the auth flow on paper before writing it in TSX, lock the design tokens before two UI sweeps. Less rebuilding, more building right the first time. That's the habit I'm taking forward.
